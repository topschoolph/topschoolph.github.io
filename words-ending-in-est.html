Answer:

Explanation:

The current COVID-19 pandemic has affected us all. The pandemic's impact and consequences, however, are felt differently depending on our status as individuals and members of society. While some try to adapt to working online, homeschooling their children, and ordering food through Instacart, others are forced to be infected in order for society to function. Our various social identities and the social groups to which we belong determine our inclusion in society and, as a result, our vulnerability to epidemics.

COVID-19 is killing a lot of people. As of October 10, 2020, more than 7.7 million people in all 50 states and four territories had tested positive for COVID-19. According to the New York Times database, at least 213,876 people have died from the virus in the United States.[1] However, these startling figures only provide half of the picture; a closer look at data by different social identities (such as class, gender, age, race, and medical history) reveals that minorities have been disproportionately affected by the pandemic. These minorities in the United States are denied their right to health.

According to the World Health Organization's report Closing the Gap in a Generation: Health Equity through Action on the Social Determinants of Health, "poor and unequal living conditions are the consequences of deeper structural conditions that together fashion the way societies are organized—poor social policies and programs, unfair economic arrangements, and bad politics."[2] I recognize that the pandemic has had and continues to have devastating effects on other minority groups, but space does not allow for an examination of the impact on other minority groups in this essay.

Using a human rights lens in this analysis allows us to translate needs and social problems into rights, focusing our attention on the larger sociopolitical structural context as the root cause of the social problems. Human rights emphasize the inherent dignity and worth of all people, who are the primary holders of rights. Governments (as well as other social actors such as corporations) bear the responsibility to respect, protect, and fulfill human rights. Human rights are inextricably linked to the societal contexts in which they are recognized, asserted, enforced, and fulfilled. Social rights, in particular the right to health, can be powerful tools for advancing people's citizenship and enhancing their ability to participate as active members of society.

Such an understanding of social rights draws our attention to the concept of equality, which necessitates a greater emphasis on "solidarity" and the "collective." Furthermore, the fulfillment of social rights is not optional in order to generate equality, solidarity, and social integration. Social policies must reflect a commitment to respect and protect the most vulnerable individuals, as well as to create the conditions for the fulfillment of economic and social rights for all in order to achieve social integration.
