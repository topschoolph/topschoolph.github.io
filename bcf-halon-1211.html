1. Why is pressurization needed for Halon 1211?


Answer:

Halons are fire extinguishing agents which are gaseous when discharged in the aircraft environment. Halons have, until recently, been in almost universal use in aircraft fire extinguishers, both portable and fixed. They exist in two forms - as Halon 1211, BromoChlorodiFluoromethane (CBrClF2) also known as 'BCF', and as Halon 1301, Bromotrifluoromethane (CBrF3).

Halon 1211 is used only in portable extinguishers and is a streaming agent

Halon 1301 is used only in fixed extinguisher installations typically cargo holds or engines and is a total flooding agent.

14. 6. Destruction oflayer occurs when chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and halons-gases formerly found in aerosol spray cans and refrigerants-are released into theatmosphere​


Answer:

Ozone depletion or ozone layer

Explanation:

Ozone depletion (example in the picture) occurs when chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and halons — gases previously found in aerosol spray cans and refrigerants — are released into the air. Synthetic compounds such as chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), hydrofluorocarbons (HCFCs) and halons destroy the ozone in the upper atmosphere (stratosphere). The stratospheric ozone layer makes life possible by protecting the earth from harmful ultraviolet (UV-B) rays generated from the sun. Ozone depletion consists of two related events observed since the late 1970s: a steady decrease of nearly four percent of the total amount of ozone in the Earth's atmosphere (the ozone layer) and a larger spring reduction in stratospheric ozone around the polar regions of the Earth. The last phenomenon is referred to as the ozone hole. There are also springtime polar tropospheric ozone depletion events in addition to stratospheric events. It is also the gradual thinning of the Earth's ozone layer in the upper atmosphere caused by the release of chemical compounds containing gaseous chlorine or bromine from industry and other human activities. The Montreal Protocol, adopted in 1987, was the first of several comprehensive international agreements enacted to stop the production and use of ozone-depleting chemicals. As a result of continued international cooperation on this issue, the ozone layer is expected to recover over time.

The ozone layer (examples in the picture) is a thin layer of Earth's atmosphere that absorbs almost all of the harmful ultraviolet light of the sun. "Ozone holes" are popular names for ozone layer damage areas. This is inaccurate. The breakdown of the osone layer is more like a really thin patch than a hole. The ozone layer or ozone shield is a region of the Earth's stratosphere that absorbs most of the Sun's ultraviolet radiation. It contains a high concentration of ozone (O3) relative to other parts of the atmosphere, although still relatively small in relation to other gases in the stratosphere. The ozone layer contains less than 10 parts per million ozone, while the average Earth's ozone concentration as a whole is about 0.3 parts per million. The ozone layer is mainly located in the lower part of the stratosphere, from approximately 15 to 35 kilometers (9.3 to 21.7 mi) above the Earth, although its thickness varies from season to season and geographically.

30. How do bromine atoms in halons react with the ozone layer?how do bromine atoms in holland's react with the ozone layer ​


Answer:

Halons are a class of chemicals that contain bromine atoms and are used as fire extinguishing agents. When halons are released into the atmosphere, the bromine atoms can react with ozone (O3) in the stratosphere, which is the layer of the Earth's atmosphere that contains most of the ozone.

The reaction between bromine atoms and ozone is similar to the reaction between chlorine atoms and ozone, which is the basis for the ozone hole. The reaction proceeds as follows:

Br + O3 → BrO + O2

In this reaction, a bromine atom (Br) reacts with ozone (O3) to form bromine monoxide (BrO) and oxygen (O2). The bromine monoxide can then react with another ozone molecule to form oxygen and bromine, which can then react with another ozone molecule to continue the cycle.

This reaction is significant because it reduces the amount of ozone in the stratosphere, which can lead to increased levels of harmful ultraviolet radiation reaching the Earth's surface. Halons are now largely banned because of their damaging effect on the ozone layer.
