Microwave is a form of electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths ranging from about one meter to one millimeter; with frequencies between 300 MHz (1 m) and 300 GHz (1 mm).[1][2][3][4][5] Different sources define different frequency ranges as microwaves; the above broad definition includes both UHF and EHF (millimeter wave) bands. A more common definition in radio-frequency engineering is the range between 1 and 100 GHz (wavelengths between 0.3 m and 3 mm).[2] In all cases, microwaves include the entire SHF band (3 to 30 GHz, or 10 to 1 cm) at minimum. Frequencies in the microwave range are often referred to by their IEEE radar band designations: S, C, X, Ku, K, or Ka band, or by similar NATO or EU designations.

15. A certain microwave has a wavelength of 0.032 meters. calculate the frequency of this microwave.


As you know, wavelength and frequency have an inverse relationship described by the equation

c= λ. f

, where

λ - the wavelength of the wave

ν - the frequency of the wave

c - the speed of light in a vacuum, approximately equal to 3⋅10^8 m/s

to solve by getting into formula:

[tex]c = λ .f \\ f = \frac{c}{λ } \\ f= \frac{3. {10}^{8}}{0.032} \\ f= 9.4 \times {10}^{9}hz[/tex]

so the frequency of microwaves is 9.4X10^9 hz.

Microwaves is one of type of electromagnetic wave.

Types of Electromagnetic Waves

1. Radio Waves

Radio waves are a form of electromagnetic radiation. These waves are formed when sound or audio turns into an electrical signal and through an oscillator wave (carrier wave) experiences modulation at a frequency contained in the radio wave frequency.

2. Microwave

Microwaves (Microwave) are electromagnetic waves with a super high frequency (Super High Frequency), which is above 3 GHz (3×109 Hz). If microwaves are absorbed by an object, the molecules and atoms in the object will vibrate, causing a heating effect.

3. Infrared waves

Infrared is an electromagnetic wave that has a longer wavelength than visible light, but shorter than radio waves. Infrared light is produced by electrons in molecules vibrating and then breaking down into different molecules as the object is heated.

4. Visible Light (red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and purple)

Visible light waves are electromagnetic waves that can be seen by the human sense of sight. Visible light waves consist of red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet.

5. Ultraviolet Waves

Ultra violet light is part of the color spectrum, but the human sense of sight cannot see it. Ultra violet light has a very short wavelength compared to other colors. This causes ultra violet light to have a much stronger penetrating power than other colors. Ultra violet light is able to penetrate human skin and can even change cell structure.

6. X-rays

X-rays have a stronger penetrating power than ultraviolet light. X-rays are able to penetrate soft structures such as flesh and wood, except for solid structures such as bone. With its ability to penetrate flesh, the bone/skeletal structure can be seen on the film screen.

7. Gamma rays

Gamma-ray waves are the strongest electromagnetic waves among other electromagnetic waves. The light wave Ү can penetrate solid structures, and can even change the atomic structure into a different atom. Ү Rays occur from a nuclear reaction process so as to produce radiation that tends to be harmful to living things.
