What do you mean by temperament and psyche?​

Temperament and Psyche

In psychology, temperament is a component of personality related with emotional dispositions and reactions, as well as their speed and intensity; the phrase is frequently used to refer to a person's prevailing mood or mood pattern. Galen, a Greek physician of the second century AD, established the concept of temperament from an older physiological idea of four fundamental bodily fluids (humours): blood, phlegm, black bile, and yellow bile. They were intended to generate temperament labeled sanguine (warm, pleasant), phlegmatic (slow-moving, apathetic), melancholy (depressed, sorrowful), and choleric (depending on their relative prevalence in the individual) (quick to react, hot tempered). More current hypotheses stress the endocrine glands' role on emotional reaction.

In modern psychology, the activity of the autonomic nervous system, particularly its sympathetic branch, is attributed fundamental importance in emotional reactivity: autonomic over-responsiveness is intimately related with neurotic tendencies. Because such reactions may be conditioned, individual differences in ease of conditioning (which is also likely inherent) have a role in temperament .
