1. A spring of negligible mass has a force constant k = 800 N/m. How far must the spring be compressed for 1.20 J of potential energy to be stored in it?


Answer:

[tex]\Large \boxed{\text{0.055\ meter}}[/tex]

Spring Potential Energy

The energy that an object has as a result of its position is known as potential energy. Stretching a rubber band increases its potential energy, commonly referred to as "stored energy". When you let go of the rubber band, this energy is converted into kinetic energy. A similar event occurs when you pull back on a bow's string before releasing the arrow to strike the target.

Elastic Potential Energy (EPE), a type of PE possessed by spring objects when force is applied and results in their deformation, is what we have in the case of the problem below.

PROBLEM

A spring of negligible mass has a force constant k = 800 N/m. How far must the spring be compressed for 1.20 J of potential energy to be stored in it?

SOLUTION

Let's write down the important details before proceeding to solve.

Given

Spring constant = [tex]k=800\ \dfrac{N}{m}[/tex][tex]EPE=U=1.20\ J[/tex]

Required

[tex]distance=\Delta x[/tex]

Unit Conversion

[tex]1\ Joule=1\ N\cdot m[/tex]

Formula

In solving the PE of springs, we will use this formula: [tex]\bold{U=\dfrac{1}{2}k\Delta x^2}[/tex], where [tex]U[/tex] refers to the magnitude of potential energy, [tex]k[/tex] is the spring constant, and [tex]\Delta x[/tex] is the change in distance between the originally uncompressed and later compressed spring.

*PE means potential energy

Solving

Substituting the given into the formula of PE and converting Joules to Newton-meter,

[tex]\displaystyle 1.20\ N\cdot m=\frac{1}{2}(800\ \frac{N}{m})\Delta x^2[/tex]

Evaluating,

[tex]\displaystyle 1.20\ N\cdot m=\left(400\frac{N}{m}\right )\Delta x^2[/tex]

[tex]\displaystyle \frac{1.20\ N\cdot m}{400\ \dfrac{N}{m}}=\Delta x^2[/tex]

[tex]1.20\ \cancel{N}m\left (\dfrac{1\ m}{400\ \cancel{N}}\right )=\Delta x^2[/tex]

[tex]\sqrt{0.003\ m^2}=\sqrt{\Delta x^2}[/tex]

[tex]\therefore\ \bold{\Delta x=0.055\ m}[/tex]

Therefore, the spring must be compressed for 0.055 m to have a potential energy of 1.20 J.

12. A tiny spring, with a spring constant of 1.20 N/m, will be stretched to what displacement by a 0.0060-N force?


Answer:

The displacement of the spring is 5 mm.

Explanation:

In our case, we are to solve for the elongation or displacement of a tiny spring stretched by a given force.

Here, we are going to use the concept of Hooke's Law and we will use the formula:

F = kx

where

F     is the force to stretch the spring, unit is in Newtons (N)

k     is the spring constant, unit is in N/m

x     is the spring elongation or displacement, unit is in meter (m)

For the given information

k = 1.20 N/m

F = 0.006 N

x = ?

Solving the problem

Let us use the formula above and substitute the given information, we have:

F = kx

0.006 N = (1.20 N/m) x

Divide both sides of the equation by 1.20 N/m, we have:

[tex]x=\frac{0.006N}{1.20 N/m}[/tex]

x = 0.005 m    or

x = 5 mm

Therefore, the spring elongation or displacement is 3 mm.

17. A spring has a constant force of 525 n/m. a force of 375 n is applied to the spring. find the elongation of the spring and its elastic potential energy. by how much will the spring elongate when stretched by a force of 350 n


Answer:

I suppose that the spring follows Hooke's Law:

F=kx,

where F is the force (it is given), k is the spring force constant (given also) and x is the length of the elongation or compression. Using this formula we can find the length of an elongation,

x=F/k.

Also it is known that for such a springs the elastic potential energy is equal to

(kx^2)/2=(F^2)/(2k).

Now perform the calculations.

For the first case, the elongation x is F/k=375/525 approx 0.71(m).

The elastic potential energy is (375^2)/(2*525) approx 134 (J).

For the second case, the elongation x is F/k=350/525 approx 0.67(m).

The elastic potential energy is (350^2)/(2*525) approx 117 (J).

There is no surprise that for the less force the elongation occurred to be less also, and the same is observed for the elastic potential energy.

Answer:

For the first question:

Given:

spring stretch, x = 0.35 m

spring constant, k = 80 N/m

To calculate:

force, F

elastic potential energy, PE

Solution:

The force required to stretch a spring is given by:

F = k * x

F = 80 N/m * 0.35 m

F = 28 N

So the force required to stretch the spring to 0.35 m is 28 N.

The elastic potential energy stored in a spring is given by:

PE = (1/2) * k * x^2

PE = (1/2) * 80 N/m * (0.35 m)^2

PE = 4.9 J

So the elastic potential energy stored in the spring when it is stretched to 0.35 m is 4.9 Joules.

For the second question:

Given:

spring stretch, x = 0.75 m

spring constant, k = 5.5 N/m

To calculate:

elastic potential energy, PE

Solution:

The elastic potential energy stored in a spring is given by:

PE = (1/2) * k * x^2

PE = (1/2) * 5.5 N/m * (0.75 m)^2

PE = 1.64 J

So the elastic potential energy stored in the spring when it is stretched to 0.75 m is 1.64 Joules.


24. The work needed to compress a spring by 40 mm is 0.80 J. How will you compute for the force constant of the spring?


Answer:

The force constant of the spring is 1000 N/m

Explanation:

Here, we are to solve for the force constant of a spring using the concept of force and work on springs.

Formula to use

W = 1/2kx²

where

W     is the work, the unit is in Joules (J)

k       is the force constant of the spring, the unit is in Newton per meter

x       is the length of the spring, the unit is in meter

Given information

x = 40 mm = 0.04 m

W = 0.80 J

k = ?

Solving the problem

Let us solve the spring constant using the given formula

W = 1/2kx²

then, substitute the given data

0.80 J = 1/2k(0.04 m)²

Apply cross-multiplication

k = [tex]\frac{0.80J(2)}{(0.04m)^2}[/tex]

k = 1000 N/m

Therefore, the force constant of the spring is 1000 N/m
