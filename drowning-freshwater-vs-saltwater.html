4. place wher freshwater meets saltwater​


Answer:

Estuaries

Explanation:

Estuaries form a unique marine biome that occurs where a source of fresh water, such as a river, meets the ocean. Therefore, both fresh water and salt water are found in the same vicinity. Mixing results in a diluted (brackish) saltwater.

An estuary is an area where a freshwater river or stream meets the ocean. In estuaries, the salty ocean mixes with a freshwater river, resulting in brackish water. Brackish water is somewhat salty, but not as salty as the ocean. An estuary may also be called a bay, lagoon, sound, or slough


5. in an estuary, how do freshwater and saltwater mix?​


Answer:

When river water meets sea water, the lighter fresh water rises up and over the denser salt water. Sea water noses into the estuary beneath the outflowing river water, pushing its way upstream along the bottom. Often, as in the Fraser River, this occurs at an abrupt salt front.

BRACKISH WATER

A supply of water that is slightly salty but not as salty as freshwater is referred to as brackish water. The level of salinity can't be determined precisely because it varies depending on the environment. Rather than a precise amount, salinity is usually measured in a range. The average salinity of freshwater sources is around 0.5 percent, while the variation in salinity ranges from 10% to 32% in most cases.

The most typical places to find brackish water are at the water's transitional spots, when fresh water joins seawater. Estuaries are the names given to these bodies of water. Man can create sources of brackish water, which are frequently found in the form of pools.
