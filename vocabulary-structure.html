22. 1. Prosody refers to the patterns of language. a. stress and intonation b. vocabulary and meaning c. grammar and structure d. sight and sound in a​


Explanation:

a. stress and intonation.

Prosody refers to the patterns of stress, intonation, and rhythm in language. It includes variations in pitch, loudness, and duration of sounds, which can convey emotions, attitudes, and other meanings beyond the literal meaning of words. Prosody is an important aspect of spoken communication as it helps to convey the speaker's intended message and emotions.

Answer:

A. stress and intonation

Explanation:

Stress and intonation are two important aspects of prosody in language.

Stress refers to the emphasis or prominence given to certain syllables or words in a sentence. In English, stress is often marked by a change in pitch, volume, or duration of the stressed syllable. For example, in the word "banana," the stress is on the second syllable, so it is pronounced as "buh-NA-na."

Intonation, on the other hand, refers to the rise and fall of pitch in a sentence. It can convey different meanings or emotions, such as a question, a statement, or sarcasm. For example, a rising intonation at the end of a sentence can indicate a question, while a falling intonation can indicate a statement or a command.

Together, stress and intonation help convey meaning and nuance in spoken language, and can greatly affect how a message is perceived by the listener.
