The M14 rifle, officially the United States Rifle, Caliber 7.62 mm, M14, is an American select-fire battle rifle that fires 7.62×51mm NATO (.308 in) ammunition. 6. would you care to fire a rifle whose bullet is ten times as massive as rhe rifle?why or why not​


Answer:

It depends on you

Explanation:

The recoil of a gun is an example of the conservation of momentum. Initially, the gun and bullet are at rest and their total momentum is zero. Ordinarily the bullet will have a small mass and a large velocity while the gun, with a much greater mass, will have a small velocity. In this question, however, the bullet is more massive than the gun. That means the velocity of the gun will be greater than the velocity of the bullet. In fact, the velocity of this gun will be ten times the velocity of the bullet.


7. who is best Sniper from the Philippines?​


Answer:

Ramon S. Subejano

Explanation:

Leaving the Philippines as a stowaway in 1927, he eventually became a merchant marine. Later purchasing a pool hall with casino winnings, he lost it during the Great Depression, then worked as a busboy until he was drafted in 1942.

World War II Edit

Pvt. Ramon S. Subejano served with Company A, 358th Infantry Regiment, 90th Infantry Division and saw combat at Normandy Beach, Ardennes, Northern France, the Netherlands, and the Rhineland. He was a scout sniper and was wounded several times. During the course of the war he earned 17 medals, credited for killing more than 400 Germans in battle. During one battle he was the sole survivor of his unit.

One of the medals was the Silver Star which cited him for "Gallantry in action on December 7, 1944 in the vicinity of Dillengen, Germany." The citation continued, "At the risk of his life, Pvt. Subejano made his way along through devastating 20 mm and machine gun fire and hand grenade explosions to the building. He then entered the strong point and going from room to room, killed five enemy, wounded six and forced the remaining 37 to surrender. At the end of the war he was flown to London for the victory parade, where he was presented to King George and Sir Winston Churchill.

Post-World War II Edit

Pvt. Ramon S. Subejano was featured in the front page of The New York Times on May 31, 1952 during the city's observance of Memorial Day. His picture appeared in the "A Salute To War Dead From A Decorated Hero" wearing his full dress army uniform complete with medals and ribbons he earned in the European and Pacific theaters rendering a salute in front of the Soldiers' and Sailors' Monument (New York). According to The New York Times, he was an unscheduled addition to the Memorial Day program when he came as a spectator in uniform with his chest full of medals and ribbons, "hobbled up the monument steps using a cane and was introduced at the speaker's stand to the crowd" He would later meet Marilyn Monroe, Senator Kennedy, and President Kennedy.

At one point his medals were stolen from his apartment; with the assistance of Senator Kennedy's office, the Department of Defense presented replacements of all his medals. In 1987, while living in the neighborhood of Brighton in the city of Boston, he was charged threatening to harm his apartment manager. Less than a year later he would die in a Veteran Affairs Hospital in New York City. Subejano is buried at Arlington National Cemetery. His story is in the Library of Congress, "One Man Army" (1978).

10. A 2.5 kg rifle fires a 50 gram bullet with a velocity of 300 m/s. Determine a) the velocity of recoil of the rifle and b) the kinetic energy that will be absorbed by the one firing the rifle.


Answer:

a. The velocity of the recoil of the rifle is -6 m/s

b. The kinetic energy of the bullet is 2250 J

Explanation:

In this problem, we are to solve for the velocity of the recoil of the rifle and the kinetic energy by firing the rifle. To do this, we will be using the concept of the conservation law and momentum. Below is the derived formula of momentum to solve for the recoil velocity.

a. Recoil velocity

Vr = - (mb ÷ mr) Vb

where

Vr     is the recoil velocity, the unit is in m/s

mb    is the mass of the bullet, the unit is in kg

mr     is the mass of the rifle, the unit is in kg

Vb     is the velocity of the bullet, the unit is in m/s

Take note that the negative sign here denotes the opposite direction of the recoil velocity to the speed of the bullet.

b. Kinetic energy

KE = ¹/₂mv²

where

KE     is the kinetic energy, the unit is in Joules (J)

m       is the mass, the unit is in kg

v        is the velocity, the unit is in m/s

Given information

mr = 2.5 kg

mb = 50 g = 0.05 kg

Vb = 300 m/s

a. Vr = ?

b. KE = ?

Solving the problem

a. First, let us solve for the recoil velocity of the rifle using the given formula

Vr = - (mb ÷ mr) Vb

Then substitute the given data

Vr = - (0.05 ÷ 2.5) 300

Vr = - 6 m/s

b. Now, let us solve for the kinetic energy of the bullet using the given formula

KE = ¹/₂mv²

Then substitute the given data

KE = ¹/₂(0.05)(300)²

KE = 2250 J

Therefore, the energy on firing the rifle is 2250 J.

21. A 5-g bullet is fired with a muzzle velocity of 200 m/s from a 5-kg rifle. With what velocity does the rifle recoil? Assume that both the bullet and the rifle are initially at rest.


Answer:

Without writing anything down you can see the gun comes in at 100x the mass of the bullet so using conservation of momentum, the quick answer is the gun would recoil with .01x the velocity of the bullet or 2m/s. And while that is probably what the teacher wants, it generally won’t be the actual recoil velocity.

Unless this is a rail gun, there is something going out the barrel in addition to the bullet. It’s probably not a lot of propellant for something this small and a bullet going this slowly but it would change the momentum equation by some amount. Normally more significant is Newton’s Third Law (the equal and opposite one) which for this problem says the recoil force would be lined up with the barrel/bullet. 26. what is webly in rifling?​


Explanation:

In firearms, rifling is machining helical grooves into the internal (bore) surface of a gun's barrel for the purpose of exerting torque and thus imparting a spin to a projectile around its longitudinal axis during shooting to stabilize the projectile longitudinally by conservation of angular momentum, improving its aerodynamic stability and accuracy over smoothbore designs.
