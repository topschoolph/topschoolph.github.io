This fallacy get its name from the Latin phrase "post hoc, ergo propter hoc," which translates as "after this, therefore because of this."
Assuming that because B comes after A, A caused B.
Of course, sometimes one event really does cause another one that comes later...
For example, if I register
For a class, and my name later appears on the roll, it's true that the first event caused the one that comes later. But sometimes two events that seem related in time aren't related as cause and event. That is, correlation isn't the same thing as causation.
Example: "President Jones raised taxes, and then the rate of the violent crime went up. Jones is responsible for the rise in crime." The increase in taxes might or might not be one factor in the rising crime rates, but the argument hasn't shown us that one caused the other :-)

16. ano ang kahulugan ng post hoc fallacy??? ​


Post hoc ergo propter hoc (Latin: "after this, therefore because of this") is an informal fallacy that states: "Since event Y followed event X, event Y must have been caused by event X." It is often shortened simply to post hoc fallacy.

The Latin phrase "post hoc ergo propter hoc" means "after this, therefore because of this." The fallacy is generally referred to by the shorter phrase, "post hoc." Examples: "Every time that rooster crows, the sun comes up. That rooster must be very powerful and important!"

23. Why is ANOVA considered as the extension of the t- and z-tests?​


ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) is considered as an extension of the t- and z-tests because it is used to compare the means of multiple groups simultaneously, while the t-test and z-test are used to compare the means of two groups only.

The t-test is used to compare the means of two groups when the sample sizes are small, and assumes that the data is normally distributed. The z-test is used to compare the means of two groups when the sample sizes are large, and also assumes that the data is normally distributed.

ANOVA, on the other hand, is used to compare the means of three or more groups. It tests the hypothesis that the means of all the groups are equal against the alternative that at least one group mean is different from the others. ANOVA can be used for both independent and dependent (repeated measures) samples, and it can also be used to detect interactions between factors.

ANOVA is considered an extension of the t- and z-tests because it uses similar principles and assumptions. In fact, the t-test and z-test can be thought of as special cases of ANOVA, where the number of groups is equal to 2.
