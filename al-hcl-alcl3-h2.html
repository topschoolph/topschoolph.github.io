BALANCED CHEMICAL EQUATIONS

- In Chemistry, a balanced equation for a chemical reaction is an equation in which the number of atoms for each element or compound in the chemical reaction including the total charge is the same for both the reactants and the products. In other words, the mass and the charge are balanced on both sides of the reaction. The law of conservation of mass in balancing a chemical equation states that no atoms are lost or generated in any chemical reactions. Instead, the atoms join together in different reactions to produce products . This is why, in a balanced symbol equation, the total number of atoms of each element is the same or equal on both sides of the equation.

3. AlCl3 + H2O arrow Al(OH)3 + HCl


Explanation:

The chemical equation you provided represents a reaction between aluminum chloride (AlCl3) and water (H2O) to form aluminum hydroxide (Al(OH)3) and hydrochloric acid (HCl).

This is a double displacement reaction where the aluminum chloride and water molecules exchange their ions to form new compounds. The aluminum chloride dissociates in water to form Al3+ ions and Cl- ions, while water dissociates into H+ ions and OH- ions.

When the Al3+ ions react with the OH- ions, they form aluminum hydroxide, which is a white precipitate. At the same time, the H+ ions react with the Cl- ions to form hydrochloric acid, which is a strong acid.

Overall, this reaction represents a neutralization reaction where an acid (HCl) and a base (Al(OH)3) react to form a salt (AlCl3) and water (H2O).

5. 1.) Sixty grams of FeCl3 reacts with 50.0 grams of H2S. 2 FeCl3 (s) + 3 H2S (g) ---> 6 HCl + Fe2S3 (s) (A) What is the limiting reactant? (B) What is the mass of HCl produced?


Solution (A):

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of each reactant.

For FeCl₃

molar mass = (55.85 g/mol × 1) + (35.45 g/mol × 3)

molar mass = 162.2 g/mol

For H₂S

molar mass = (1.008 g/mol × 2) + (32.07 g/mol × 1)

molar mass = 34.086 g/mol

Step 2: Determine the mole ratios needed.

Using FeCl₃

mole ratio = 2 mol FeCl₃ : 6 mol HCl

Using H₂S

mole ratio = 3 mol H₂S : 6 mol HCl

Step 3: Calculate the number of moles of HCl produced by each reactant.

Using FeCl₃

[tex]\text{moles of HCl = 60.0 g FeCl₃} × \frac{\text{1 mol FeCl₃}}{\text{162.2 g FeCl₃}} × \frac{\text{6 mol HCl}}{\text{2 mol FeCl₃}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of HCl = 1.1097 mol}[/tex]

Using H₂S

[tex]\text{moles of HCl = 50.0 g H₂S} × \frac{\text{1 mol H₂S}}{\text{34.086 g H₂S}} × \frac{\text{6 mol HCl}}{\text{3 mol H₂S}}[/tex]

[tex]\text{moles of HCl = 2.934 mol}[/tex]

Step 4: Determine the limiting reactant.

Since FeCl₃ produces less number of moles of HCl

[tex]\boxed{\text{FeCl₃ is the limiting reactant.}}[/tex]

Solution (B)

Step 1: Calculate the molar mass of HCl.

molar mass = (1.008 g/mol × 1) + (35.45 g/mol × 1)

molar mass = 36.458 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the mass of HCl produced.

Since FeCl₃ is the limiting reactant

[tex]\text{mass of HCl = 1.1097 mol HCl} × \frac{\text{36.458 g HCl}}{\text{1 mol HCl}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\text{mass of HCl = 40.46 g}}[/tex]

12. Magnesium reacts with hydrochloric acid (HCl) to produce Magnesium chloride ( MgCl2 ) and hydrogen H2 gas (a) How many moles of H2 can be formed by the reaction 0 2.46 moles of HCl with magnesium .How many grams H2 can be formed by the reaction of 0.568 mole of Mg with sufficient HC Mg (s) + 2HCl (aq) ------> MgCl (aq) + H2 (g)


Answer:

(a) 1.23 moles of H2
(b) 1.144 grams of H2 gas

Explanation:

The balanced chemical equation for the reaction of Magnesium with Hydrochloric acid is:

Mg (s) + 2HCl (aq) → MgCl2 (aq) + H2 (g)

(a) To determine the number of moles of H2 gas formed by the reaction of 2.46 moles of HCl with Magnesium, we need to use the stoichiometric coefficients of the balanced equation. From the balanced equation, we can see that 1 mole of Magnesium reacts with 2 moles of Hydrochloric acid to form 1 mole of Hydrogen gas. Therefore, the number of moles of H2 gas formed is:

2.46 moles HCl × (1 mole H2/2 moles HCl) = 1.23 moles H2

Therefore, 1.23 moles of H2 can be formed.

(b) To determine the mass of H2 gas formed by the reaction of 0.568 moles of Mg with sufficient HCl, we again need to use the stoichiometric coefficients of the balanced equation. From the balanced equation, we can see that 1 mole of Magnesium reacts with 1 mole of Hydrogen gas to form MgCl2. Therefore, the number of moles of H2 gas formed is also 0.568 moles.

Now, we can use the molar mass of Hydrogen to determine the mass of H2 gas formed:

0.568 moles H2 × 2.016 g/mol = 1.144 g H2

Therefore, 1.144 grams of H2 gas can be formed.

a. ANSWER:

Al(OH)₃ + 3HCl → AlCl₃ + 3H₂O

b. SOLUTION:

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of Al(OH)₃ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 3 moles of HCl.

The molar masses of Al(OH)₃ and HCl are 78.00 g/mol and 36.46 g/mol, respectively.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of HCl} & = \text{0.500 g} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3}{\text{78.00 g} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3} \times \frac{\text{3 mol HCl}}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3} \times \frac{\text{36.46 g HCl}}{\text{1 mol HCl}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{0.701 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

c. SOLUTION:

• For grams of AlCl₃

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of Al(OH)₃ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 1 mole of AlCl₃.

The molar mass of AlCl₃ is 133.3 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{AlCl}_3 & = \text{0.500 g} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3}{\text{78.00 g} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3} \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{AlCl}_3}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3} \times \frac{\text{133.3 g} \: \text{AlCl}_3}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{AlCl}_3} \\ & = \boxed{\text{0.854 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Based on the balanced chemical equation, 1 mole of Al(OH)₃ is stoichiometrically equivalent to 3 moles of H₂O.

The molar mass of H₂O is 18.02 g/mol.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of} \: \text{H}_2\text{O} & = \text{0.500 g} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3 \times \frac{\text{1 mol} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3}{\text{78.00 g} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3} \times \frac{\text{3 mol} \: \text{H}_2\text{O}}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3} \times \frac{\text{18.02 g} \: \text{H}_2\text{O}}{\text{1 mol} \: \text{H}_2\text{O}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{0.347 g}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

d. SOLUTION:

[tex]\begin{aligned} \sum mass_{\text{reactants}} & = \sum mass_{\text{products}} \\ \text{mass of} \: \text{Al(OH)}_3 + \text{mass of HCl} & = \text{mass of} \: \text{AlCl}_3 + \text{mass of} \: \text{H}_2\text{O} \\ \text{0.500 g + 0.701 g} & = \text{0.854 g + 0.347 g} \\ \text{1.201 g} & = \text{1.201 g} \: \checkmark \end{aligned}[/tex]

Chemical Reactions

Involves rearrangement of the molecular or ionic structure of a substance. Process in which one or more reactants are converted to one or more different products. Can be classified into five basic types. Analyzing reactants and products will allow placing it into one of these categories. Some reactions will fit into more than one category.

Types of Chemical Reactions:combination/synthesisdecompositionsingle replacement/single displacementdouble replacement/double displacementcombustion

A combination reaction, also known as a synthesis reaction, is a reaction in which two or more substances combine to form a single new substance. Combination reactions can also be called synthesis reactions. One combination reaction is two elements combining to form a compound.

A decomposition reaction is a reaction in which a compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. Most decomposition reactions require an input of energy in the form of heat, light, or electricity.

A single replacement reaction is a reaction in which one element replaces a similar element in a compound. A metal must be replaced by another metal while a non - metal must be replaced also by a non - metal.

A double replacement reaction is a reaction in which the positive and negative ions of two ionic compounds exchange places to form two new compounds. Double-replacement reactions generally occur between substances in aqueous solution. In order for a reaction to occur, one of the products is usually a solid precipitate, a gas, or a molecular compound such as water.

to balance an equation you need to know the following terms:

Coefficient which is a number that is written before the formula or the symbol of an element in an equation.

Subscript which is a number that is written slightly below a symbol of element.

in balancing equations you need to follow the following steps

1. write the unbalanced equation

2. list all elements present in the equation

3. identify and list the number of atoms of each element in both reactant side and the products side

4. write an appropriate coefficient that will balance the number of atoms of each element in the equation, this can be done looking at the subscript and giving a correct number that when multiplied by the subscript will balance the number of atoms of the element in both reactants and product.

Note: you cannot change the subscript because it will change the chemical compound in the equation.
