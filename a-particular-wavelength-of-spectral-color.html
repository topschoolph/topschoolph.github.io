16. The spectral lines of a star are observed to be shifted toward larger wavelengths.


The spectral lines of a star are observed to be shifted toward larger wavelengths. This shows that the star is receding from us.

Star spectrum formation

Spectrum patterns of stars are generally different, in 1863 an astronomer named Angelo Secchi grouped the spectrum of stars into 4 groups based on the similarity of the arrangement of the spectral lines.

Miss A. Maury of the Harvard Observatory found that Secchi's classification can be sequenced continuously until the spectrum of a star with the previous sequence star does not differ much. The classification made by Miss Maury was later revised by Miss Annie J. Cannon. Until now the Miss Cannon classification is used.

Star Motion

Stars are not stationary, but move in space. The movement of this star is very difficult to follow because the distance is very far, so we see the star as if it has remained in place since the past until now.

The rate of change in the angle of a star's position is called its proper motion. True motion is usually symbolized by and is expressed in arcseconds per year. The star with the greatest true motion is Barnard's star with = 10”.25 per year (in 180 years this star has shifted only as wide as the full moon).
