1. How many formula units of Na2CO3 are required to produce 100 grams of NaNO3?


Answer:

Explanation:

2 Na2CO3 + 2 HNO3 → NaNO3 + CO2 + H2O

The molar mass of Na2CO3 is 105.99 g/mol, and the molar mass of NaNO3 is 84.99 g/mol.

To calculate the number of formula units of Na2CO3 needed to produce 100 g of NaNO3, we can use the following steps:

Convert the mass of NaNO3 to moles using its molar mass:

100 g NaNO3 ÷ 84.99 g/mol = 1.176 mol NaNO3

Use the mole ratio between Na2CO3 and NaNO3 from the balanced equation to determine the number of moles of Na2CO3 needed:

2 mol Na2CO3 ÷ 1 mol NaNO3 = 2 mol Na2CO3 needed per 1 mol NaNO3

2 mol Na2CO3/1 mol NaNO3 x 1.176 mol NaNO3 = 2.352 mol Na2CO3 needed

Convert the moles of Na2CO3 to formula units:

2.352 mol Na2CO3 x 6.02 x 10^23 formula units/mol = 1.415 x 10^24 formula units of Na2CO3 needed

Therefore, 1.415 x 10^24 formula units of Na2CO3 are required to produce 100 grams of NaNO3.

17. When baking soda (NaHCO3) is heated, carbon dioxide (CO2) is released. This makes bread, cookies and other pastries to rise. If 42.0 g of NaHCO3 was used, how much CO2 was released? (NaHCO3 = 84.00 g/mol, CO2 = 44.01, Na2CO3 = 105.99 g/mol, H2O = 18.02 g/mol)


Explanation:

The chemical reaction when baking soda is heated is:

2 NaHCO3 → Na2CO3 + CO2 + H2O

According to the balanced chemical equation, for every 2 moles of NaHCO3 that is heated, 1 mole of CO2 is produced. We can use this ratio to determine how much CO2 is produced from 42.0 g of NaHCO3.

First, we need to calculate the number of moles of NaHCO3 in 42.0 g:

42.0 g / 84.00 g/mol = 0.5 mol

Now we can use the mole ratio to determine the number of moles of CO2 produced:

0.5 mol NaHCO3 × (1 mol CO2 / 2 mol NaHCO3) = 0.25 mol CO2

Finally, we can calculate the mass of CO2 produced using the molar mass of CO2:

0.25 mol CO2 × 44.01 g/mol = 11.0 g CO2

Therefore, 11.0 g of CO2 is released when 42.0 g of NaHCO3 is heated.

Hence, the volume of 1.2 M Na₂CO₃ needed is 1.9 L.

26. consider the reaction: NaCl + AgNo3 = AgCl + NaNo3. If 52 g of salt reacts with silver nitrate, what mass in grams of silver chloride will be produced?​


Answer:

127.5 grams of AgCl will be produced

Explanation:

The given are the mass of NaCl of 52 grams and the chemical equation providing the relationship of one mole NaCl reacting to AgNO3 producing one mole of AgCl and NaNO3.

Step 1: Get the molar mass of NaCl and AgCl.

NaCl = 22 98 g/mol (Na) + 35.45 g/mol (Cl) = 58.43 g/mol

AgCl = 107.86 g/mol (Ag) + 35.45g/mol(Cl) = 143.31 g/mol

Step 2: Equate the relation of silver nitrate's mass and reacting moles in the given chemical equation to silver chloride's resulting mass and number of moles with their respective molar mass.

52 g NaCl x [tex]\frac{1 mol}{58.43g} NaCl[/tex] x [tex]\frac{1 mole of AgCl}{1 mole of NaCl}[/tex] x [tex]\frac{143.31 grams}{1 mole} AgCl[/tex] = 127.5 grams of AgCl

Therefore, 127.5 grams of silver chloride will be produced after 52 grams of salt react with silver nitrate.
