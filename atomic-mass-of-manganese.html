2. Manganese crystallizes in a face-centered cubic system. The volume of a manganese atom is 9.21 x 10-24 cm3. 1. What is the radius of one manganese atom? (Hint: The volume of a spherical ball of radius r is V=4/3 πr^3.) 2. What is the edge length of the unit cell? 3. What is the volume of manganese atoms in one unit cell? 4. What is the mass of manganese atoms in one unit cell? 5. What is the density of manganese?


1. What is the radius of one manganese atom?Solution:

[tex]V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^{3}[/tex]

[tex]r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{3V}{4\pi}}[/tex]

[tex]r = \sqrt[3]{\frac{3(9.21 × 10^{-24} \: \text{cm}^{3})}{4\pi}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{r = 1.30 × 10^{-8} \: \text{cm}}[/tex]

2. What is the edge length of the unit cell?Solution:

Since manganese has FCC structure

[tex]a = 2\sqrt{2}r[/tex]

[tex]a = 2\sqrt{2}(1.30 × 10^{-8} \: \text{cm})[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{a = 3.68 × 10^{-8} \: \text{cm}}[/tex]

3. What is the volume ofmanganese atoms in one unit cell?Solution:

[tex]V = a^{3}[/tex]

[tex]V = (3.68 × 10^{-8} \: \text{cm})^{3}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{V = 4.98 × 10^{-23} \: \text{cm}^{3}}[/tex]

4. What is the mass of manganese atoms in one unit cell?Solution:

Since manganese has FCC structure, there are 4 atoms in a unit cell. The atomic weight of manganese is 54.94 g/mol.

[tex]m = 54.94 \: \frac{\text{g}}{\text{mol}} × \frac{\text{1 mol}}{6.022 × 10^{23} \: \text{atoms}} × \frac{\text{4 atoms}}{\text{1 unit cell}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{m = 3.65 × 10^{-22} \: \text{g}}[/tex]

5. What is the density of manganese?Solution:

[tex]\rho = \frac{m}{V}[/tex]

[tex]\rho = \frac{3.65 × 10^{-22} \: \text{g}}{4.98 × 10^{-23} \: \text{cm}^{3}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\rho = 7.33 \: \text{g/}\text{cm}^{3}}[/tex]

Answer:

Manganese dioxide is a black powder that catalyzes the breakdown of hydrogen peroxide to water and oxygen. Which of the following statements is correct? c. The particles in the catalyzed reaction will have more energy than in the uncatalyzed reaction.

Explanation:

Manganese dioxide catalyzes the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide to oxygen  and water. Since manganese dioxide is a catalyst, it is not consumed in the reaction.  Normally the activation energy for this uncatalyzed reaction is around 75 KJ/mole  (Moelwyn-Hughes). With the addition of the synthesized catalyst, MnO2, we can  actually lower this activation energy down to about 58 KJ/mole (Moelwyn-Hughes)  and that speeds up the decomposition by 1073 times at 200C.  

k = A e -58,000/8.3145 x 293 catalyzed

k = A e -75,000/8.3145 x 293 uncatalyzed

k 17,000/8.3145 x 293 catalyzed / k uncatalyzed = e = 1073

Conclusion:

The rate constant of the catalyzed v. uncatalyzed has increased by 1,073 times.  Therefore, the rate of catalyzed is 1073 times the rate of the uncatalyzed.

20. Manganese is a transition element essential for the growth of bones. What is the mass in grams of 3.22 x 10^20 Manganese atoms, the number found in 1kg of bone?


✏️MASS OF A SUBSTANCE

[tex]\sf{\large{\red{STRATEGY:}}}[/tex]

The conversion sequence used to convert atoms of Mn to grams of Mn is

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{number of Mn atoms} \rightarrow \text{moles of Mn} \rightarrow \text{grams of Mn} \end{aligned}[/tex]

[tex]\sf{\large{\red{SOLUTION:}}}[/tex]

Step 1: Determine the conversion factor used to convert atoms of Mn into moles of Mn.

Note that there are 6.022 × 10²³ Mn atoms in 1 mole of Mn.

[tex]\dfrac{\text{1 mol Mn}}{6.022 \times 10^{23} \: \text{Mn atoms}}[/tex]

Step 2: Determine the conversion factor used to convert moles of Mn into grams of Mn

Note that there are 54.94 grams of Mn in 1 mole of Mn.

[tex]\dfrac{\text{54.94 g Mn}}{\text{1 mol Mn}}[/tex]

Final Step: Calculate the mass of Mn by using dimensional analysis.

[tex]\begin{aligned} \text{mass of Mn} & = 3.22 \times 10^{20} \: \cancel{\text{Mn atoms}} \times \frac{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol Mn}}}{6.022 \times 10^{23} \: \cancel{\text{Mn atoms}}} \times \frac{\text{54.94 g Mn}}{1 \: \cancel{\text{mol Mn}}} \\ & = \boxed{\text{0.0294 g Mn}} \end{aligned}[/tex]

Therefore, the mass of 3.22 × 10²⁰ atoms of Mn is 0.0294 g.
